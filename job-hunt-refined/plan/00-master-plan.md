## This week — admin & bases-covered

These are the time-sensitive items. Knock them out so they stop taking up mental space.

- **Unemployment** — Filed. ✅ Watch for any follow-up requests (they sometimes ask for separation details). Respond promptly so payments aren't delayed. If they contact the bank and the bank contests, that's where the termination letter matters.
- **Termination letter** — Requested, arriving in 10–14 days by mail. When it shows up, read it carefully for the *stated reason*. The exact language matters for unemployment eligibility and for any lawyer conversation. File it somewhere safe.
- **Lawyer calls** — One firm declined (not their area). Call 3–4 more tomorrow. Frame it as a *consultation on whether anything is actionable* — not "I want to sue." You're looking for: (a) is there any retaliation/discrimination angle given how the report happened, (b) is there severance leverage worth pursuing, (c) anything you should avoid saying in the Wednesday dispute call. Most do free or cheap consults. If 3–4 all say the same thing, you have your answer and can stop spending energy here.
- **Reconstruct your documentation** — You got logged out fast, so write down now, while it's fresh: the date, who was on the term call (boss + manager), what was said, the employee-relations number, the dispute/appeal you opened, and the Wednesday callback. A plain timeline doc. This is your record since you have no email access.

## Wednesday — dispute / appeal call

Going in realistic, just covering bases. Keep it factual and unemotional — you're building a record, not winning an argument.

Notes to have in front of you:
- The account was **not under your real name**.
- **No affiliation to the bank** anywhere on it — not named, not linked, not identifiable as an employee.
- It was **personal, off-duty conduct**, separate from work, and never affected your job performance.
- The report came from an **ex-girlfriend** — worth noting neutrally if they ask how it surfaced, since the motive/context may matter.
- Ask directly: is there a formal appeal process, what's the timeline, and is severance on the table given length of service.

Don't argue or get defensive even if it feels unfair. Get answers, take notes, end the call.

## Plan A — the job hunt (main effort)

This is where the real energy goes. The honest gap you named: you weren't learning current-market skills at BofA. So the first phase is partly upskilling, partly applying — done in parallel, not sequentially. Don't disappear into prep for two months; apply while you sharpen.

### Phase 1 (Weeks 1–3): set the base

- **Resume rewrite** — Lead with the Microsoft ML background and Cornell CS, then BofA backend. Frame BofA in terms of systems scale and reliability, not legacy stack. Target language toward trading firms / fintech / market-adjacent eng, which is where you already had momentum.
- **LeetCode** — Start now, steady cadence. Mediums daily, a few hards a week. Track patterns (arrays/strings, hashmaps, two-pointer, trees, graphs, DP). Aim for ~100–150 over the hunt, not a number this week.
- **System design refresher** — You'd built foundational knowledge here before for trading-firm interviews. Dust it off: databases, caching, queues, the usual.
- **Pick 1–2 portfolio projects** that signal market-proximity and modern tooling. You already have real material to draw on — the crypto market-data monitoring system (Hyperliquid funding rates / OI / volume), the funding-rate dashboard, the Telegram nag bot. Polish one into something presentable on GitHub rather than starting cold.

### Phase 2 (Weeks 2–8): apply in volume, keep sharpening

- Start applications by week 2 — don't wait until you "feel ready," because that day never arrives and interviews *are* the prep.
- Reactivate the conversations you already had going (you'd been talking to trading firms / hedge funds / fintech; the Noetica-type backend-full-stack roles).
- Track every application in a simple sheet: company, role, date, stage, contact.
- Tune the LeetCode/system-design mix toward whatever shows up in your actual interview loops.

### Phase 3: closing

- Negotiate. With cash runway you're not desperate, which is leverage — use it.
- Don't take the first thing out of relief. The whole point of this reset is to land somewhere better, not just somewhere.

## Lease / Chicago — decide deliberately, not reactively

You've been chewing on this for months, so it's real — but it's not this-week urgent, and you don't want to torch cash *or* make a panic move.

- **Map the numbers**: monthly rent + parking, months left on the lease, and any early-termination / sublet clause. Get the actual figures in front of you.
- **The key dependency**: where you land for work shapes where you live. A new job might be remote, might be in Chicago, might be elsewhere. Deciding the lease before you know that is putting the cart first.
- **Reasonable sequence**: run the job hunt from where you are now → once you have an offer or a clear direction, *then* decide whether to break the lease, sublet, or ride it out. If the lease is up for renewal soon anyway, that decision may make itself.
- **Cash discipline**: you have ample cash and don't want to burn it. Keep a number in your head for how many months of full-burn runway you're comfortable spending before it's a problem. That number is your real deadline, and it's almost certainly months out — which is why none of this is an emergency.
